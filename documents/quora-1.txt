Originally Answered: What tips and things can you tell me before I plan on doing CCNA?
This is what I did to get my Cisco CCNA. I took the 2 exam option, a year or two apart since I work & have an active social life, as opposed to the 1 exam option, and I am currently repeating this process for CCNP (the next level certification to CCNA) before my CCNA in Routing & Switching expires in end of September 2018. CCNA lasts 3 years.

I do tend to over prepare since the exam cost is around $300 (CCNP), I found the Cisco exams harder then the Microsoft exams that I took for MCSE 15 plus years ago.

I did the following in this order:

Training: in person, online, books -I found Training material from class easier to digest to the official Cisco Press Books. If you can get work to pay for training, books, and exam, the better. I took in person Training from Global Knowledge for 1st exam and online training for the 2nd exam from INE. If you do the 2 exam option, passing the 1st exam makes you a CCENT.

Hands on experience: I did on the job networking for nearly a year before my 1st CCNA class which helped immensely. In order to learn the items I did not yet do, I used “Boson” CCNA product for virtual exercises based on coworker recommendation who already had a CCNA. Training centers also give you this virtual exercise option, some for a few months after a class (Global Knowledge), some are based yearly subscriptions like INE. I think Boson cost me around $100, look for specials after you get on their mailing list.

Exam Cram Book (does not exist for CCNP), read cover to cover, memorize short cut card.

Brain Dumps (google ccna brain dumps), and practice Exams in the few weeks before the exam. Exam Cram book includes a practice exam.

If you don’t pass your first exam, like me, do not beat yourself up over it, it sucks to lose $$$, but use the score card to find out your weak areas for study, but note you do have to schedule RE-TAKE OF EXAM AT LEAST A WEEK in the future. There was no, I will come back tomorrow and retake it like other vendors. However, the good news is now you will now know the exam taking drill on the next attempt.

Check your exam requirements for Certification with Cisco, they did change while I was working on my CCNA.

I hope this helps, Good luck!

-Neil

Focus Focus Focus.

Since you made your question plural, I assume you are taking the ICND1 first, then ICND2 after. If that is so, then make sure you focus on what is important to each section.

When you are studying for the first exam, make sure you also study IP Subnetting as dedicated as any other portion. When you get to test 2, you should stop worrying about the stuff you learned in part 1. It will not carry over. You will not be asked to answer questions across the lines here.

So, focus on each section individually and completely while you are prepping. Know how to subnet in your head. Know CIDR.

That’s all I can really say about it, other than best of luck and do great.

That being said, my answer to your question is this. Do not try to memorize the questions but understand each and every question, answer, and distracting non-answer.

You can augment this with youtube, forums, google, etc. I did several certs like this. But after awhile it becomes exhausting and not fun. Mostly, because I was no longer doing it for the thrill of learning but to keep up with expiring certs or new contract requirements.

How far can you take your CCNA?

CCNA will be your first step for a rewarding IT career. Now, beware of the word IT career. It is very specific to a certain set of IT skills that are in demand in the IT industry, such as-

Routing and Switching
Collaboration
Voice
Service Provider
Security
Data Center etc.,
You can only take CCNA so far. It is just an aggregation of basic technical skills that you need to step into a particular segment of the IT world. You must take your CCNA to the next step with a CCNP or a CCIE in the same or an alternate segment.

It is even more beneficial for today’s IT industry to complement your IT skills acquired through CCNA/CCNP/CCIE with other in-demand IT skills certifications like CompTIA+, VMWare, Microsoft, RHCP etc.,

What exactly is needed to completely study CCNA?

I have done my CCNA in Routing and Switching. So, let me tell you my learning path. All you really need to get up to speed is to get your hands on Todd Lamle CCNA R&S book, and read it cover to cover. This will give you a solid foundation on what you can expect from CCNA certification.

If you have some money to spare, you can take INE CCNA R&S course by Brian McGahan or CBT Nuggets CCNA R&S course by Keith Barker. They will give you a better perspective over the topics in CCNA and can make you explore the course deeper.

By this time, you should be ready to take the exam head-on. But, you can refer Cisco Offical Press Books on CCNA. This resource works better compared to either Todd Lamle or video courses. However, Cisco press books are written somewhat dryly compared to Todd Lamle, for CCNA level certification.

Hope this helps!

Yes, you can study CCNA at home. CCNA provides fundamental networking knowledge, which can be acquired from the comfort of your home. There is a lot of study material available online, from YouTube to Udemy, from books to dumps, and whatnot.

You can study from any given resource, be it from YouTube channels such as PyNet Labs, which offers free CCNA training videos, or Udemy paid courses, books, etc. However, it is advised not to stay dependent on one source and diversify your knowledge with additional sources. You must also remember that despite being a basic networking course, CCNA holds great value, and your CCNA knowledge will be crucial to decide your networking future. Therefore, it is suggested to stay focused and dedicated while you are training for CCNA.

Therefore, It can be said that CCNA training can be done from home, but the question is:

Is It worth the Effort?

Well, practically speaking, it isn't worth it because CCNA only consists of 40% theoretical part, and the rest, 60%, is practical training done on Cisco Packet Tracer. So, you will come across many network engineers suggesting to go for training with an institute because that will provide a more focused curriculum with advanced practical training.

However, the Cisco software, Packet Tracer, is available for free online and can be downloaded on any system having at least 2 GB of RAM. So, training from home can be worthwhile if you practice your labs and learn everything side by side. However, that will take more time than training with an Institute.

Considering the cost of CCNA training in India, which is around INR 10k, I suggest going for CCNA training because your time will be utilized better, your knowledge will be better, and you get to clear your doubts during the training. It will reap better rewards for you, and you will have more chances of succeeding when you attempt the CCNA Certification.

Many institutes are offering CCNA training; you can check out their curriculum to find the best institute for yourself. You can also take a sneak peek at PyNet Labs' CCNA training in India page to know more about CCNA training.

To conclude this answer, I am adding one more thing that will help you decide which is better for you. If you want basic CCNA knowledge to move on to other networking tracks, you can do it from your home; however, if you are looking to attempt CCNA certification in future or want to build a networking career with CCNA knowledge. In that case, CCNA training with an institute becomes mandatory for you.
